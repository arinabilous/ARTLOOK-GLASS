    Thinking of getting your own glass floor? Here are some considerations.

    Installation

    Glass floors are installed with a carefully engineered support structure.
    One of the biggest challenges is to ensure that the support structure is the proper depth, so the glass is flush with the floor—it isn’t a material that can be shaved down on site

    Finishes

    Glass floors are relatively easy to customize with lighting, translucent or colored layers, cast glass for an old-fashioned look, or a graphic design embedded into the layers.
    And, yes, it is possible to see up someone’s skirt through a clear glass floor, so some homeowners opt for a frosted look for modesty or privacy.

    Materials

    Usually, glass floors are made of tempered, laminated glass held together with polyvinyl butyral, or PVB, the same binder as in car windshields. Altogether, the glass is usually about 1¼-inches thick, but you’ll need it thicker if you want a bigger span of glass without support beams.

    A bigger safety concern is the slipperiness of the glass, which compared with polished marble. Homeowners can add a textured coating, although it can affect the transparency of the glass. Glass floors can also get scratched, much like a hardwood floor, except that the scratches are more visible. It is also worth making sure you use low-iron glass to avoid a greenish tint.
